Desired dough temperature — water temp calculator

What it does

Calculates the exact water temperature needed to hit your target dough temperature (DDT), using the true thermal mass of every ingredient rather than the simplified 3- or 4-factor shorthand.

The formula

Each ingredient contributes heat proportional to its mass × specific heat capacity (Cp):
T_water = (DDT × total_heat_capacity − Σ(mass_i × Cp_i × T_i)) / (m_water × Cp_water)

Where Cp values used: water = 4.18, flour = 1.80, starter = 3.20 (mix of flour+water), salt/other = 0.88 J/g·K. The friction factor accounts for heat generated by machine mixing (0 for hand mixing).

Why it's more accurate than the shorthand

The 3/4-factor shorthand assumes all ingredients have equal thermal influence. In reality water has ~2.3× the heat capacity of flour per gram, so the water temperature matters more than the shorthand implies. For high-hydration doughs this matters: a 75% hydration dough has more water weight, so water temp errors propagate more strongly.

Sensitivity

The ±1°C water sensitivity tells you how much the final dough temp shifts per 1°C error in your water temperature. Typically 0.3–0.45°C/°C for 70–80% hydration.

Dough warmup

What it does

Models how dough temperature approaches room temperature over time, accounting for both the dough's thermal mass and the bowl's thermal mass (which starts at room temperature).

Two-body thermal model

The dough and bowl exchange heat with each other and with the room air simultaneously:
dT_dough/dt = k_db(T_bowl−T_dough)/(m_d·Cp_d) + k_da(T_room−T_dough)/(m_d·Cp_d)
dT_bowl/dt = k_db(T_dough−T_bowl)/(m_b·Cp_b) + k_ba(T_room−T_bowl)/(m_b·Cp_b)

This correctly models that a room-temperature ceramic bowl initially warms cold dough, then both approach room temp together. Numerically integrated at 1-minute steps.

Bowl presets

Each preset has a specific mass, heat capacity (Cp), and surface heat transfer coefficient to room air. Covered bowls have lower air exchange. The three curves shown are the three presets simultaneously — the highlighted one corresponds to your selected preset.

Why it matters

A heavy ceramic bowl at room temp acts as a heat reservoir that speeds up warmup for cold dough by up to 30%. Ignoring this (old single-body model) underestimates warmup speed.

Starter peak estimator

What it does

Estimates when your starter will peak after being taken out of the fridge (or from any starting temperature), accounting for the time it takes to warm up.

Temperature model

The starter in a glass jar warms following Newton's Law of Cooling with τ = 195 min (200g glass jar + 100g starter, still air). The warmup curve is T(t) = T_room − ΔT·e^(−t/τ).

Q10 fermentation kinetics

Instead of assuming fermentation rate is proportional to temperature (linear), the Q10 model uses the biological reality that reaction rates follow Arrhenius-like exponential scaling:
r(T) = Q10 ^ ((T − T_ref) / 10)

With Q10 = 2.0 this means rate doubles every 10°C. At 14°C (10°C below a 24°C reference) the rate is 0.5×; at 34°C it would be 2×. This is well-validated for sourdough yeast in the 10–35°C range.

Fermentation progress = ∫r(T(t))dt, and the starter peaks when this integral equals 1 (= what the same starter takes at T_ref for peak_hours).

Calibrating to your starter

The most important input is "peak time at reference temp". Feed your starter, hold it at your reference temp throughout, and time when it peaks (dome + starts falling). Use that as your calibration point. The Q10 model will then extrapolate correctly to warming curves.

Q10 sensitivity

Q10 varies slightly by strain and temperature range. 1.8–2.2 covers most sourdough starters. Lower Q10 → rate is less temperature-sensitive → shorter estimated warmup penalty.
