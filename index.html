Desired dough temperature — water temp calculator

Purpose

This tool calculates the water temperature required to achieve a target final dough temperature (DDT) after mixing. All inputs are treated as a weighted thermal mixture, accounting for the true heat capacity of each ingredient.

Model

Each ingredient contributes thermal energy proportional to its mass and specific heat capacity (Cp). The dough reaches DDT when the weighted sum of all ingredient temperatures equals the target:

T_water = (DDT × ΣHC − Σ(HC_i × T_i)) / HC_water

where HC_i = mass_i × Cp_i. Specific heat values used:

IngredientCp (J/g·K)
Water4.18
Flour1.80
Starter (100% hydration)3.20 (interpolated)
Salt / dry additions0.88

The starter is internally split into its flour and water components based on its hydration percentage, and the water contribution from the starter is subtracted from the total formula water.

Friction factor

Machine mixing converts mechanical energy to heat, raising dough temperature above what the ingredient temperatures would predict. The friction factor compensates for this by lowering the effective DDT target before solving for water temperature. Typical values:

Mixing methodFriction factor (°C)
Hand mix / stretch-and-fold0
Stand mixer, slow speed (5–8 min)3–5
Stand mixer, medium speed (8–12 min)6–9
Spiral mixer, slow speed2–4
Spiral mixer, fast speed5–8
High-speed commercial mixer10–20

Friction factors vary by dough hydration, mixing bowl shape, and ambient temperature. The values above are starting estimates; calibrate by measuring actual dough temperature after mixing and adjusting accordingly.

Bowl temperature contribution

The optional bowl input accounts for the thermal mass of the mixing vessel, which starts at room temperature and exchanges heat with the dough during mixing. For a stainless steel bowl (~350 g), this can shift final dough temperature by 0.5–1.5°C depending on dough mass and mixing duration. Enable this input when precision is critical or the bowl is notably warmer or cooler than the dough ingredients.

Sensitivity

The ±1°C sensitivity readout shows how much the final dough temperature shifts per 1°C error in water temperature. This is determined purely by the water fraction's share of total heat capacity. Higher hydration doughs have higher sensitivity; a 75% hydration dough typically shows ~0.38°C/°C.

Dough warmup time after mixing

Purpose

Models how the temperature of mixed dough approaches room temperature over time, accounting for the separate thermal masses of the dough and the mixing bowl.

Two-body thermal model

The dough and bowl are treated as two coupled thermal bodies, each exchanging heat with the other and independently with the surrounding air:

dT_dough/dt = [k_db·(T_bowl−T_dough) + k_da·(T_room−T_dough)] / (m_d·Cp_d)

dT_bowl/dt = [k_db·(T_dough−T_bowl) + k_ba·(T_room−T_bowl)] / (m_b·Cp_b)

The bowl starts at room temperature. k values are interface-specific heat transfer coefficients (W/K). The system is integrated numerically at 1-minute steps.

Why bowl mass accelerates warmup (for cold dough)

A bowl at room temperature represents a heat reservoir in direct contact with the dough. A heavy ceramic bowl at 25°C pressed against 18°C dough transfers substantial heat in the first minutes — far faster than convection from room air alone. This effect is proportional to bowl mass × Cp and the dough-bowl contact coefficient. A 900 g ceramic bowl contains roughly 756 J/K of thermal energy available to warm the dough, compared to ~40 J/K for a thin metal bowl. The ceramic bowl therefore warms cold dough faster initially, then converges to room temperature more slowly because its own large mass takes longer to equilibrate with room air. For warm dough in a cool room, the ceramic bowl similarly acts as a stronger heat sink.

Cold starter warmup & peak time

Purpose

Estimates the time to peak activity for a sourdough starter that begins fermentation at a temperature different from its eventual equilibrium temperature — for example, after removal from refrigeration.

Temperature model

The starter in a glass jar warms according to Newton's Law of Cooling:

T(t) = T_room − (T_room − T_start) · e^(−t/τ)

with τ = 195 min, empirically estimated for a 200 g glass jar containing 100 g starter in still air. This is a single-body approximation; actual warmup will vary slightly with jar thickness and air circulation.

Q10 fermentation kinetics

Biological reaction rates — including yeast fermentation — scale exponentially with temperature rather than linearly. The Q10 model captures this:

r(T) = Q10 ^ ((T − T_ref) / 10)

where r is the relative fermentation rate, T_ref is the calibration temperature, and Q10 is the temperature sensitivity coefficient. A Q10 of 2.0 means the fermentation rate doubles with each 10°C increase. This is well-supported for Saccharomyces cerevisiae and common sourdough LAB strains in the 10–35°C range. At temperatures below ~8°C the model will overestimate activity slightly, as enzymatic processes approach their thermal minima.

Cumulative fermentation progress is computed as:

P(t) = ∫₀ᵗ r(T(τ)) dτ

Peak is reached when P(t) = peak_hours — the integral at constant T_ref over the reference peak duration.

Calibration

The reference peak time must be measured with the starter held at a constant, known temperature throughout the entire feed cycle — not warming into it. Feed the starter, place it in a temperature-controlled environment or water bath at the reference temperature, and record the time from feeding to peak (dome collapses or float test passes). This calibration point anchors all estimates.

Q10 sensitivity

Reported Q10 values for mixed sourdough cultures range from approximately 1.8 to 2.3. A lower Q10 means the rate is less temperature-sensitive, which shortens the estimated penalty for cold starts. When in doubt, Q10 = 2.0 is the standard assumption for sourdough yeast-driven rise.

Starter peak time by feeding ratio

Purpose

Estimates the time from feeding to peak activity for a sourdough starter based on feeding ratio, temperature, and flour type. Also calculates how much to feed to reach a target yield.

Peak time model

Peak times are calibrated to Summit Sourdough's comprehensive data at 68°F (20°C) for six feeding ratios (1:1:1 through 1:10:10). Temperature scaling uses the Q10 model with Q10 = 2.0:

time(T) = time(20°C) / 2^((T−20)/10)

The Bulk-O-Matic System is a nine-factor tool to help determine the optimal end of bulk fermentation. The system was developed by Tom Cucuzza for the Tartine Basic Country Bread recipe. View all nine indicators together as a “constellation” of data — no single variable decides it.

Dough temp

Target 78–82°F (25.5–28°C). Measure at center with a probe thermometer. Average across the whole bulk, not just the end. Below target slows fermentation; above breaks down gluten and risks overproofing.

Room temp

Ambient temperature affects how fast the dough warms (or cools). Useful context alongside dough temp.

Rise %

Measure volume increase in a straight-walled vessel. Target 25–35% for the standard recipe. Adjust targets for lower dough temps (e.g., 75–100% at 21°C). Most rise happens in the last 15–20% of bulk.

Domed on top

Look for a “shoulder” around the edges. Early false dome comes from the last stretch & fold. True dome = CO2 buildup. A flattening dome signals overproofing.

Bubbles on top

4–8 large bubbles = active fermentation. Thin bubble membranes = nearing overproofing. Deflated bubbles = overproofed.

Bubbles on sides

Look through the vessel walls. High density of small/medium bubbles = healthy. Shrinking number or size = overproofing.

Wobble

Shake the bowl gently. Underproofed = stiff / little movement. Sweet spot = subtle “splashing” against sides. Overproofed = liquefied, shiny surface.

Windowpane

Stretch a piece of dough thin. Thick & stiff = needs more time. Strong translucent membrane = good. Weak / tears easily = gluten breaking down (overproofing).

Smell

Progresses: floury → ripe (fermenting fruit) → sweet → acidic → starter-like. The sweet fragrant stage is the sweet spot.

Based on the Bulk-O-Matic System by Tom Cucuzza — The Sourdough Journey
